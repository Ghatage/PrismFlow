The Cursor analogy is the right one to push hard, because Cursor's success wasn't "chat next to code" — it was three specific mechanics: deep context of your project, diffs you approve before they apply, and inline completion. Map those to video and you get your must-have list almost mechanically.
1. Timeline diffs (the single most important one). When the agent does something — "tighten the pacing in act 2," "replace scene 3 with a night version" — it must not mutate your timeline. It proposes a changeset: ghost clips rendered semi-transparent on the timeline, accept/reject per change, like Cursor's green/red blocks. This is the feature that makes people trust an agent with their edit. Without it, agent-editing feels like Russian roulette; with it, you've reinvented the review loop that made AI coding actually adopted. If you build only one novel thing, build this.
2. Project context / indexing. Cursor indexes your codebase; your editor indexes the project. Every clip carries provenance metadata — prompt, model, seed, params, parent asset — plus derived context: narration transcript, a one-line vision-model description per shot, character/style references used. That index is what the chat composer sends to the LLM. It's the difference between "generate a fox" and the agent knowing your fox, your palette, your established shot grammar. It also gives you semantic search for free ("find the shot where the fox jumps") which is a great 10-second demo moment.
3. Prompt-as-source regeneration. Since everything has provenance, right-click any clip → edit its prompt, re-roll seed, or "same prompt, different model." Clips aren't files; they're cached evaluations of prompts. This is your fal-native story too: the model field on each clip is literally a fal endpoint, and "search fal for a better model for this clip" becomes a context-menu action. fal's Sandbox concept (same input across multiple models) belongs inside the editor: select a clip, "compare across 3 models," pick the winner into the timeline.
4. Character and style locking. From an AI filmmaker's perspective this is the #1 pain point, full stop — consistency across shots is 35% of even the media track's judging rubric because everyone struggles with it. So: an asset bin of locked characters/styles (reference images, fal Assets under the hood), and every generation the agent makes is forced through those references. "Cast" a character once, use them everywhere. If your demo shows the same character coherent across 5 generated shots, judges will notice.
5. Draft/final quality tiers. Generations cost money and minutes. The editor should default every agent generation to fast/cheap/low-res draft mode for iteration, with a single "final render" pass that regenerates hero shots at quality + upscales. Show a live credits meter. This sounds boring but it's what makes the tool plausible as a product (25% user-value criterion) — nobody edits with 4-minute feedback loops.
6. The agent pane as an async job queue, not a spinner. You already have this instinct with the left pane. Make it real: generations are long-running jobs, so agents work in parallel while you keep editing, results land as pending diffs when done. Bonus if the agent speculatively generates — while you're reviewing scene 2, it's already drafting variations of scene 3. That's the "tab completion" of video editing: the editor is always one step ahead of you.
7. Script view bound to the timeline. Descript proved text-based video editing works. Have a script/storyboard document as a first-class citizen where each beat maps to timeline scenes bidirectionally — edit the narration text, the TTS and captions regenerate; delete a paragraph, the scene drops. For your learning-cartoon demo this is perfect: the lesson script is the document, and non-editor users (parents, teachers) live entirely in that view while power users drop to the timeline.
If I had to cut for 72 hours solo: 1, 2, 3 are the thesis (diffs + context + provenance — that's "Cursor for video" and nobody's nailed it), 4 is the demo wow, 5–7 are stretch. The double-shift chat composer is fine but it's table stakes — the diff mechanic is what makes your demo different from "I put a chatbot next to Remotion."
One warning from the technical-execution angle (35%): make the agent's tool calls visible in that left pane — "searched fal registry → picked kling-2.5 → generated 3 candidates → composed diff." Judges reward seeing the machinery, and it converts your fal-native architecture from a claim into a demo.
